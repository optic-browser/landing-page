Go Dark Checklist

This is a practical progression: start with low-friction wins, then move into stronger compartmentalization. Most people can cut their tracking surface by 60% to 80% without leaving normal life.

Phase 1: Same day upgrades

These changes are fast and reduce cross-site profiling right away.

1. Lock account settings

Disable ad personalization and activity history where possible. Use each platform's privacy dashboards to delete and auto-delete historical activity.

2. Harden your browser

Use strict tracking protection, block third-party cookies, disable unnecessary browser telemetry, and run a content blocker by default.

3. Split your identities

Keep separate browser profiles for work, personal life, shopping, and research. This breaks many cross-context behavioral links.

4. Turn on MFA + passkeys

Strong account security prevents takeover events that can expose years of private content and metadata.

Phase 2: Reduce metadata leakage

Messaging hygiene

Prefer end-to-end encrypted messengers for sensitive communication, review disappearing message settings, and remove cloud backups when threat model requires it.

Impact: high

Network hygiene

For routine privacy, use trusted VPN providers. For stronger anonymity needs, use Tor Browser for separate tasks and do not log in to identity-linked accounts in that session.

Impact: medium-high

Device permission audit

Remove microphone, camera, contacts, and precise location access from apps that do not need it. Re-check monthly.

Impact: medium

Phase 3: Near-anonymous operating mode

Compartmented personas

Use isolated email aliases, separate phone numbers, and distinct payment methods for each identity context. Never cross-log those contexts.

Minimize real-name coupling

Avoid reusing profile photos, bios, usernames, and writing signatures across platforms when operating a high-privacy profile.

Threat-model first

Full anonymity has usability costs. Choose controls based on your risks: harassment, stalking, data brokerage, political exposure, or targeted scams.

Evidence and references

These recommendations are based on security guidance from major providers and digital-rights organizations.
